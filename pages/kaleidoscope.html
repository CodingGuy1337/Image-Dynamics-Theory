Kaleidoscope lens
Your mind is a kaleidoscope with rules.

A kaleidoscope takes a messy scene, folds it through mirrors, and produces a pattern. Your brain does something similar: it takes messy sensory input, folds it through memories, beliefs, and goals, and produces “what the world feels like right now.”

The simple picture

Imagine your mind holds a shifting picture called Z. IDT says the picture changes because your mind is constantly trying to make a few things true at once:

  • Match what you're seeing (sensory fit).
  • Predict what happens next (reduce surprise).
  • Keep intentions coherent (act like one agent).
  • Keep relationships consistent (cause, social meaning, object permanence).
When constraints fight, the pattern “snaps” into a new arrangement. That snap is what a mental shift feels like.

Slices: what you notice

A slice is the part of the picture that dominates awareness. When you're hungry, the slice favors food. When you're embarrassed, it favors social signals.

  • Orientation: which way the mind is pointed (problem-solving, social defense, curiosity).
  • Radius: how intense the slice is (how much bandwidth it steals).

Liminality: the “rearrange me” slider

Liminality is how willing the kaleidoscope is to rotate. Low liminality: the pattern locks and you stay stubborn. High liminality: mirrors spin and new meanings appear, but you may lose stability.

“Going with the flow” is usually medium liminality: enough flexibility to adapt, not enough to dissolve.

Reflection: not poetry, just bookkeeping

A good reflection mentions: the situation, the action, what was expected, what happened, and what that implies for next time. In the model, it's a decoded summary of a trajectory.
